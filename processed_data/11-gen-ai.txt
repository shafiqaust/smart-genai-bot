PAGE 5 — Energy-Based Models (EBM) — Boltzmann Distribution
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Energy-Based Models (EBM)

Boltzmann distribution formula:
  p(x) = e^{-E(x)} / ∫_{x̃ ∈ X} e^{-E(x̃)}

Components:
  - p(x): probability of data point x
  - E(x): energy function evaluated at x (numerator uses negative energy)
  - Denominator: partition function Z = ∫ e^{-E(x̃)} dx̃ — integral over ALL possible x in the data space X
  - The denominator normalizes the distribution so probabilities sum to 1

Energy function: E(x)
  - Maps a data point x to a scalar energy score
  - Low E(x) → high probability
  - High E(x) → low probability

Historical context:
  - Introduced in 1868 by Ludwig Boltzmann
  - Originally used to describe gases in thermal equilibrium
  - Adopted in machine learning as the basis for EBMs

PAGE 7 — Two Major Problems
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Two major problems with EBMs

Problem 1: Computational difficulty — the partition function is INTRACTABLE
  - The denominator ∫_{x̃ ∈ X} e^{-E(x̃)} requires integrating over ALL possible data configurations
  - This is computationally impossible in high-dimensional spaces
  - Solution approach: Contrastive Divergence (bypasses the need to compute Z directly)

Problem 2: How to generate new data given a trained model?
  - We cannot directly sample from p(x) because Z is intractable
  - Solution approach: Langevin Dynamics (iterative sampling method)

Summary mapping:
  - Intractable partition function → solved by Contrastive Divergence
  - Sampling / generation → solved by Langevin Dynamics

Visual: The formula p(x) = e^{-E(x)} / ∫ e^{-E(x̃)} is annotated with a red circle around the denominator to highlight the intractability problem.

PAGE 9 — Langevin Dynamics: Smooth Landscape Required
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Navigating the landscape requires rolling hills, not sheer cliffs

Left side — ReLU activation:
  - Produces a JAGGED energy landscape (orange/red)
  - Problem: "A jagged landscape suffers from vanishing gradients and mathematical blind corners. It is impossible to roll down smoothly."
  - ReLU is piecewise linear — non-differentiable at kinks

Right side — Swish activation:
  - Produces a SMOOTH energy landscape (blue/teal)
  - Advantage: "Swish ensures the terrain is continuous, allowing the model to find the valley floor."
  - Formula: swish(x) = x · sigmoid(x) = x / (e^{-x} + 1)

Key insight: EBMs use Swish (or similar smooth activations) instead of ReLU because Langevin dynamics requires computing gradients of the energy function w.r.t. the INPUT — a smooth, differentiable landscape is essential.

PAGE 11 — Langevin Dynamics: The Update Rule
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Langevin Dynamics provides the gravity

Update rule (Langevin dynamics equation):
  x^k = x^{k-1} - η · ∇_x E(x^{k-1}) + ω

Where:
  - x^k: updated sample at step k
  - x^{k-1}: sample from previous step
  - η (eta): step size / learning rate
  - ∇_x E(x^{k-1}): gradient of the energy function w.r.t. the INPUT x (NOT the weights θ)
  - ω (omega): stochastic noise term (prevents getting trapped in local minima)

Annotations on slide:
  - "Calculate the negative gradient of the energy function with respect to the input image (not the weights)."
  - "Take a small step downhill."
  - "Noise" (pointing to ω)

Key distinction: The gradient is taken w.r.t. x (input), not θ (model weights). Weights are FROZEN during generation.

Visual: Energy landscape with a white zigzag path showing the noisy descent from high energy to low energy valley.

Source attribution on slide: NotebookLM

PAGE 12 — Langevin Dynamics: Role of Noise
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Injecting noise prevents getting trapped

Problem without noise:
  - "Without stochastic noise, gradient descent would get permanently stuck in local minima—generating corrupted, half-formed images."

Role of noise term ω:
  - ω ~ N(0, σ) — sampled from a Gaussian distribution with mean 0 and std σ
  - "The random noise term 'bounces' the sample out of shallow traps."

Three-step visualization:
  1. Ball is stuck at a local minimum (red region) — trapped
  2. Noise is injected: ω ~ N(0, σ) — ball is "bounced" (shown with explosion effect)
  3. Ball escapes and rolls toward the true global minimum (blue valley)

Key insight: Stochastic noise is essential to Langevin dynamics — it prevents premature convergence to suboptimal local minima and ensures proper exploration of the energy landscape.

PAGE 13 — The Paradigm Shift (EBM vs Standard Training)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: The Paradigm Shift
Subheading: Both use gradient descent, but the EBM generation phase applies it to entirely different entities. We are optimizing the data itself.

Comparison table:

| Aspect            | Standard Neural Network Training | EBM Generation (Langevin)     |
|-------------------|----------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| What is updated?  | The Network Weights (θ)          | The Input Image (x)           |
| What is fixed?    | The Input Image (x)              | The Network Weights (θ)       |
| What is minimized?| The Loss Function                | The Energy Score E(x)         |

Summary:
  - Training phase: update θ (weights), fix x (data), minimize loss
  - Generation phase: fix θ (weights), update x (sample), minimize E(x)
  - This is the core conceptual inversion that defines EBM generation

PAGE 15 — Contrastive Divergence: The Intractability Problem
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: The impossibility of measuring every grain of sand
Annotation on slide: "The energy function does not output probability"

Problem statement:
  - To calculate true probability using Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE), we must divide the energy score by the partition function Z:
    Z = ∫ e^{-E(x)} dx
  - This integral represents the energy of ALL possible observations in the sample space
  - In a 32x32 image space (1,024 dimensions), this is computationally IMPOSSIBLE
  - "We cannot calculate the altitude of every possible configuration of pixels."

The intractable integral:
  Z = ∫ e^{-E(x)} dx

Key point: Because the energy function outputs a raw score (not a normalized probability), we cannot directly use MLE — we cannot compute Z.

PAGE 16 — Contrastive Divergence: The Solution
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Contrastive Divergence: Sculpting the local terrain

Solution concept:
  "Instead of mapping the entire infinite landscape, we only adjust the terrain locally."

Historical origin:
  - Proposed by Geoffrey Hinton in 2002
  - Contrastive Divergence bypasses the intractable denominator by only measuring the difference between real and fake samples

Core idea:
  - Instead of computing the full partition function Z, compare:
    → Energy score of REAL data samples (from training set)
    → Energy score of FAKE/generated samples (from Langevin sampling)
  - Adjust weights to LOWER energy of real samples and RAISE energy of fake samples

Visual: 3D terrain with two colored points — a red/orange spike (real data, high energy being pushed down) and a blue/teal valley (fake data, low energy being pulled up), with two compass-like measuring tools marking the two locations.

Source attribution on slide: NotebookLM

PAGE 17 — Contrastive Divergence: Loss Function
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Push the real down, pull the fake up

Loss function definition:
  L = E(x_real) - E(x_fake)
  "The loss function is simply the difference between the energy scores of the real and fake observations. Maximize the contrast."

Two training objectives:
  Left side (REAL data — digit "5"):
    - Action: "Force energy scores of real data toward zero."
    - Visual: A pressing/stamping machine pushing the energy surface DOWN at the real data point

  Right side (FAKE data — digit "4"):
    - Action: "Force energy scores of generated fakes higher."
    - Visual: A piston pushing the energy surface UP at the fake data point

Training signal:
  - The gradient of L w.r.t. weights θ tells the model how to reshape the energy landscape
  - Goal: real data → low energy valley; fake data → high energy peak

PAGE 18 — Note: Contrastive Divergence Implementation Details
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Note

Key implementation notes:

1. Contrastive divergence requires computing the difference between energy scores of real and fake samples, used as the loss function.

2. Problem: To calculate energy scores of fake samples, we would need to sample EXACTLY from p(x) — which is not possible (Z is intractable).

3. Alternative / practical solution:
   a. Use Langevin sampling to generate a set of observations with low energy scores — approximated by running a SMALL number of steps (not until convergence).
   b. Maintain a REPLAY BUFFER of samples from previous iterations to use as the starting point for the next batch.
   - Code reference: generate_sample() function

Key design decisions:
  - Run only a few Langevin steps per training iteration (not full convergence)
  - Use replay buffer to warm-start Langevin chains (efficiency)

PAGE 19 — Replay Buffer
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: The Replay Buffer establishes a basecamp

Problem:
  "Running Langevin dynamics from pure noise takes too many steps to be practical during training."

Solution — Replay Buffer:
  - "The Buffer saves previously generated fakes, providing a 'basecamp' partway down the mountain."
  - "We resume sculpting from there."
  - Buffer capacity: max 8,192 observations

Buffer mechanics (two intake sources):
  Intake 1 (95% of batch): Pulled from the existing Buffer (warm-started samples — already partially descended)
  Intake 2 (5% of batch): Generated from pure noise (ensures diversity, prevents mode collapse)

Flow:
  Pure Noise → (5%) + Buffer (95%) → Mixed batch → Run Langevin steps → NEXT TRAINING BATCH → Updated samples returned to Buffer

Key insight: The replay buffer makes contrastive divergence tractable during training by avoiding full Langevin chains from scratch each iteration.

PAGE 21 — MNIST EBM Architecture
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: Building the MNIST Architecture
Subheading: A series of stacked convolutional layers compress a 1,024-dimensional pixel space into a solitary energy score.

Network architecture:
  Input Layer:   32 x 32 x 1  (grayscale image, 1,024 dimensions)
  ↓
  Conv2D (16 filters)   — Swish activation
  ↓
  Conv2D (32 filters)   — Swish activation
  ↓
  Conv2D (64 filters)   — Swish activation
  ↓
  Conv2D (64 filters)   — Swish activation
  ↓
  Output Layer:  Dense(1) — single scalar energy score E(x)

Design choices:
  - Stacked Conv2D layers with increasing filter depth: 16 → 32 → 64 → 64
  - Swish activation throughout (required for smooth gradients during Langevin sampling)
  - Final Dense(1) layer outputs a single scalar — the energy score
  - No softmax, no classification head — purely a regression to a scalar

PAGE 24 — The Dual Engine of EBMs
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: The Dual Engine of Energy-Based Models
Subheading: Training an EBM requires two concurrent processes: one to define the rules of gravity, and one to play the game.

Two components working together:

LEFT ENGINE — The Sculptor: Contrastive Divergence
  - Action: Modifies the Weights (θ)
  - Goal: Shape the terrain (define the energy landscape)
  - How: Adjusts θ so real data has low energy and fake data has high energy

RIGHT ENGINE — The Explorer: Langevin Dynamics
  - Action: Modifies the Input (x)
  - Goal: Navigate the terrain (generate samples)
  - How: Iteratively moves x downhill in the energy landscape using gradient steps + noise

Interaction:
  - Langevin generates fake samples → used by Contrastive Divergence as negative examples
  - Contrastive Divergence updates weights → reshapes landscape → changes what Langevin navigates
  - Cycle repeats → progressively better energy landscape and better samples

Visual: Infinity-loop diagram connecting the two engines bidirectionally.

PAGE 25 — History of EBM and Connection to Diffusion Models
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
HEADING: A bit of history of EBM and looking towards the next …
Section title on slide: From energy landscapes to modern diffusion

Historical timeline:

  1868: Boltzmann distribution (physics — gases in thermal equilibrium)
  1980s: Boltzmann Machines (early neural EBMs with discrete units)
  2000s: Contrastive Divergence EBMs (Geoffrey Hinton, 2002 — made training practical)
  Today: Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) — DALL-E 2, Imagen

Evolutionary connection:
  "The principles of Energy-Based Models—navigating data space with stochastic gradients and estimating scores—evolved into a technique known as score matching. This framework laid the direct foundation for today's state-of-the-art Diffusion Models."

Key lineage:
  EBMs (Langevin dynamics + score functions)
  → Score Matching
  → Score-Based Generative Models
  → Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs)
  → Modern image generation: DALL-E 2, Imagen
